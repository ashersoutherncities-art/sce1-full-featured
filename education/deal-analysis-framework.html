Complete Deal Analysis Framework

📖 18 min read 📝 2,500+ words 👤 By Darius Walton

Here's the reality: 90% of new investors skip proper deal analysis and end up underwater. They fall in love with a property, trust the seller's numbers, or rely on "gut feel." Then they wonder why their cash flow is negative and they can't refinance.

Deal analysis isn't optional. It's the difference between building wealth and funding someone else's retirement.

This guide walks through the exact framework I use to evaluate every property—from ARV calculation to cash flow projections to ROI metrics. Use it, customize it, but most importantly: actually run the numbers before you write a check.

The Deal Analysis Process (High-Level)

Every analysis follows the same structure:

  1. Determine After Repair Value (ARV) — What's it worth fixed up?
  2. Estimate Repair Costs — What does it cost to get there?
  3. Calculate Maximum Allowable Offer (MAO) — What's the most you can pay?
  4. Project Operating Income & Expenses — What's the cash flow?
  5. Calculate ROI Metrics — Does this meet your investment criteria?
  6. Stress Test — What if things go wrong?

Let's break down each step.

Step 1: Determining After Repair Value (ARV)

ARV is what the property will be worth after renovations. This isn't a guess—it's data-driven.

How to Calculate ARV

Method 1: Comparable Sales (Most Common)

  1. Pull recently sold comps (last 3-6 months)
    • Same neighborhood (within 0.5 miles)
    • Similar square footage (±15%)
    • Same bed/bath count
    • Similar condition (renovated)
  2. Identify 3-5 strong comps
  3. Calculate average price per square foot
  4. Apply to your subject property

Example: ARV Calculation

Comp 1 (123 Oak St) $185,000 / 1,200 SF = $154/SF
Comp 2 (456 Elm Ave) $192,000 / 1,250 SF = $154/SF
Comp 3 (789 Pine Dr) $198,000 / 1,300 SF = $152/SF
Average Price/SF $153/SF
Subject Property (1,220 SF) $186,660 ARV

Method 2: Agent/Appraiser Opinion

Ask an agent for a "comparable market analysis" (CMA) or hire an appraiser for a desktop valuation. Cost: $0 (agent) or $150-300 (appraiser).

💡 Pro Tip: Be Conservative

If comps range from $150-160/SF, use the lower end ($150). Better to underestimate ARV and be pleasantly surprised than overestimate and lose money.

Common ARV Mistakes

  • Using list prices instead of sold prices — Sellers can list at any price; sales reflect reality
  • Cherry-picking comps — Don't ignore lower sales just because you want a higher ARV
  • Ignoring market trends — Declining market? Factor in 3-5% depreciation
  • Comparing apples to oranges — A 1,500 SF house with a pool ≠ your 1,200 SF teardown

Step 2: Estimating Repair Costs

This is where most investors blow it. Underestimate repairs by 20%, and your "great deal" becomes a money pit.

How to Estimate Repairs

Option 1: Contractor Walkthrough (Most Accurate)

Bring a licensed contractor to the property. Get a written estimate. Budget an extra 15-20% contingency.

Option 2: Per-Square-Foot Method (Quick Estimate)

  • Light cosmetic: $15-25/SF (paint, flooring, fixtures)
  • Moderate rehab: $25-40/SF (kitchen, bath updates, some structural)
  • Heavy rehab: $40-60/SF (full gut, roof, HVAC, foundation)
  • Complete rebuild: $60-100+/SF

Example: Repair Estimate (1,220 SF House)

Kitchen remodel $12,000
2 Bathroom updates $8,000
Flooring (LVP throughout) $4,500
Interior paint $3,000
New HVAC system $6,500
Exterior paint $2,500
Landscaping $1,500
Electrical/plumbing repairs $2,000
Subtotal $40,000
Contingency (20%) $8,000
Total Repair Budget $48,000

⚠️ Hidden Cost Traps

Always budget for:

  • Permit fees (if required)
  • Dumpsters and waste removal
  • Utilities during renovation
  • Holding costs (mortgage, taxes, insurance)
  • Unexpected structural issues (once walls are open)

Step 3: Maximum Allowable Offer (MAO)

Now that you know ARV and repair costs, calculate the most you can pay and still hit your profit target.

The 70% Rule (Fix-and-Flip)

MAO = (ARV × 0.70) - Repairs

This ensures a 30% margin for profit, holding costs, financing, and unexpected issues.

Example: MAO Calculation

ARV $186,660
× 70% $130,662
- Repairs -$48,000
Maximum Allowable Offer $82,662

If the seller wants $95,000? Walk away. The numbers don't work.

💡 Adjusting the 70% Rule

Hot markets: May need to go to 75-80% and accept thinner margins
Rental hold strategy: Less critical—focus on cash flow instead

Step 4: Cash Flow Analysis (For Rentals)

If you're holding the property as a rental, forget ARV. Focus on monthly cash flow.

Rental Income Projection

How to Determine Market Rent

  1. Check Zillow, Apartments.com, Rentometer for comparable rentals
  2. Call property managers and ask what similar units rent for
  3. Drive the neighborhood and note "For Rent" signs with prices

Be realistic. If comps rent for $1,200-1,400, budget $1,200. Never assume top-of-market rents.

Operating Expenses

Don't just subtract the mortgage and call it cash flow. Here's what you actually pay:

Operating Expense Categories

  • Property taxes: Get exact figure from county assessor
  • Insurance: Call for quotes (typically $800-1,500/year for SFH)
  • Property management: 8-10% of gross rents (even if self-managing—budget for it)
  • Maintenance & repairs: 10% of gross rents (ongoing)
  • Vacancy: 5-8% of gross rents (even great tenants leave eventually)
  • CapEx reserve: 5-10% for big-ticket items (roof, HVAC, water heater)
  • HOA fees: If applicable
  • Utilities: If owner-paid (water/sewer is common for multifamily)

Example: Cash Flow Analysis

Income
Gross Rent $1,300/month
Expenses
Mortgage (PITI) -$750
Property Management (8%) -$104
Maintenance (10%) -$130
Vacancy (5%) -$65
CapEx Reserve (8%) -$104
Monthly Cash Flow $147
Annual Cash Flow $1,764

The 1% Rule (Quick Filter)

The 1% Rule

Monthly Rent ≥ 1% of Purchase Price

Example: $100,000 property should rent for $1,000/month minimum

Reality check: The 1% rule is increasingly hard to hit in hot markets. It's a screening tool, not a deal-killer. Focus on actual cash flow.

Step 5: ROI Metrics (Does This Meet Your Criteria?)

Cash flow is great, but how does this property perform relative to your investment? Three key metrics:

1. Cash-on-Cash Return (CoC)

Cash-on-Cash Return

CoC = Annual Cash Flow ÷ Total Cash Invested

Example Calculation

Purchase Price $120,000
Down Payment (25%) $30,000
Closing Costs $3,000
Repairs $15,000
Total Cash Invested $48,000
Annual Cash Flow $1,764
Cash-on-Cash Return 3.7%

Target CoC: 8-12% minimum for rental properties. Below that? You're better off in index funds.

2. Cap Rate (Capitalization Rate)

Cap Rate

Cap Rate = Net Operating Income (NOI) ÷ Property Value

NOI = Gross Rent - Operating Expenses (exclude mortgage)

Cap rate measures the property's return independent of financing. Useful for comparing properties across markets.

4-6%

Class A markets (stable, low growth)

6-8%

Class B markets (moderate risk/return)

8-12%

Class C/D markets (higher risk, higher return)

3. Internal Rate of Return (IRR)

IRR accounts for time value of money—cash flow over multiple years plus appreciation at exit. More complex, typically calculated in Excel or specialized software.

When to use it: Syndications, multiyear holds, complex capital structures. Overkill for single-family buy-and-holds.

💡 Which Metric Matters Most?

Buy-and-hold: Cash-on-Cash Return
Comparing markets: Cap Rate
Syndications/funds: IRR

Step 6: Stress Testing Your Deal

The numbers look good on paper. But what if things go wrong?

Run These Scenarios

1. What if repairs run 30% over budget?

Recalculate cash flow or flip profit. Still work? If not, negotiate a lower purchase price or walk.

2. What if vacancy is 15% instead of 5%?

Can you cover the mortgage for 2 months with reserves? If not, the deal is too tight.

3. What if rent drops 10%?

Economic downturn, oversupply, or tenant turnover can lower rents. Does your cash flow go negative?

4. What if interest rates rise before refinancing?

For BRRRR strategies, can you still pull your cash out if rates are 2% higher?

⚠️ The Break-Even Test

If your deal only works under perfect conditions, it's not a deal. Build in cushion. Markets change. Tenants suck sometimes. Contractors disappear. Your analysis should survive reality.

✅ Pre-Offer Checklist

  • ARV calculated using 3+ comparable sales
  • Repair estimate from licensed contractor (or conservative SF calculation)
  • MAO calculated (70% rule or custom)
  • Cash flow projection includes ALL expenses (not just mortgage)
  • Cash-on-Cash return meets minimum target (8-12%)
  • Stress-tested for 30% cost overrun and 15% vacancy
  • Exit strategy defined (flip, refinance, long-term hold)
  • Financing pre-approved or in place

Common Deal Analysis Mistakes

1. Emotional Attachment

"This property has so much potential!" Yeah, and so does the lottery. Stick to the numbers.

2. Trusting Seller-Provided Numbers

Seller says rents are $1,500? Verify independently. Always.

3. Ignoring Market Fundamentals

Even a "good deal" in a declining market can lose money. Check population growth, job growth, crime trends.

4. Underestimating Time

Flips take longer than planned. Refinances get delayed. Cash flow doesn't start until the property is rented. Budget for time.

5. Analyzing Too Many Properties, Offering on None

Analysis paralysis is real. Set criteria, stick to it, make offers. You'll learn more from one closed deal than 100 analyzed.

Final Thoughts

Deal analysis isn't glamorous. It's spreadsheets, comps, contractor quotes, and conservative assumptions. But it's the difference between investors who build wealth and those who tell cautionary tales at meetups.

Run the numbers. Every time. No exceptions.
